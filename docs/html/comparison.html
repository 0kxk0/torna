vs Alternatives

A clear-eyed look at the closest things on Solana today. Each one is the right answer for some workload. None of them is the right answer for the workloads Torna targets.

spl-account-compression

The official Solana primitive for compressed state. The backing structure is a concurrent Merkle tree, optimized for the cNFT use case where you have millions of leaves but only care about membership and updates.

Strengths. Massive scale. Millions of leaves in tens of kilobytes of on-chain state. Battle-tested at the largest cNFT collections (some with tens of millions of mints). The standard Solana team's primitive.

Weaknesses. No order. The tree gives you set semantics — you can prove a leaf exists, you can update a leaf, you can add or remove leaves. You can't ask "what's the smallest leaf?" or "give me all leaves in this range." Reads require off-chain Merkle proofs supplied by an indexer.

Verdict. Different problem. If your use case is "huge registry, point queries only" — cNFTs, allowlists, identity registries — compression is the right call and Torna is overkill. If you need order, compression doesn't help.

Phoenix slab (Phoenix V1)

Ellipsis Labs' orderbook DEX uses a custom slab allocator packed into a single 10 MB Solana account. The allocator is hand-tuned for matching: every order is a slot in the slab, kept in a doubly-linked list sorted by price.

Strengths. Extremely tight. CU costs are excellent because there's no path validation, no PDA derivation, no CPI — just direct indexing into the same account's bytes. For a single-market orderbook where the team controls the entire stack, it's hard to beat.

Weaknesses. All writes contend on one account. Every order placement, every cancel, every fill locks the same writable account. Solana's scheduler runs them one at a time. Phoenix's busiest markets bottleneck on exactly this.

The 10 MB account cap is the other hard wall. At Phoenix's encoding, that's around a few million open orders per market. For most current markets, fine. For long-tail markets at scale, not enough.

Verdict. Optimized for one specific use case. Not a primitive other programs can plug into. If your protocol needs Phoenix's data structure, you can't borrow it — you have to either fork Phoenix or rewrite.

OpenBook v2

Similar shape to Phoenix in spirit. A single-account slab allocator per market, written in Rust, with the orderbook data structure compact and tightly tuned.

Same set of tradeoffs as Phoenix: extremely efficient when the workload fits the design, no parallelism within a single market, hard cap at the account size limit, not reusable as a generic primitive outside OpenBook itself.

Where Torna actually wins

Two specific properties only Torna offers, today, as a reusable Solana primitive:

Sorted, range-scannable state spread across accounts

No other on-chain primitive on Solana lets you ask "give me every entry in [X, Y] in ascending order, even if those entries live across dozens of separate accounts." spl-account-compression is unordered. Phoenix is sorted but trapped in a single account. Range scan over leaves is the operation a real DEX, marketplace, or analytics product needs and nothing else provides as a generic primitive.

Parallel writes to disjoint regions

Single-account designs serialize every write through the master account. Torna parallelizes them across leaves. Devnet result: 6 inserts in 1 slot vs 6 inserts in 6 slots, a 6.47× speedup. As an integrator, that's the difference between hitting Solana's network ceiling and hitting the data structure's ceiling.

Where Torna loses

Equally important to be honest about:

  • Pure point-membership at huge scale. spl-account-compression is cheaper if your only need is "is X in this set." A million-entry Merkle tree costs orders of magnitude less than a million-entry B+ tree.
  • Single-market hyper-optimized matching. Phoenix's slab beats Torna on raw CU when the workload fits inside one account and you don't need parallelism. The team that wrote Phoenix burned years tuning that path; Torna's average insert at 5,823 CU is good for a generic primitive but not best-in-class for the single-account use case.
  • No Anchor ergonomics yet. Torna ships an IDL, but the canonical client is direct TypeScript or direct Rust. If your integration relies on Anchor codegen and IDL-driven everything, that's not a frictionless story today.

When to reach for Torna

When all three of these are true for your workload:

  1. You need ordered keys (range queries or sorted iteration are core to your product).
  2. You expect state to either outgrow a 10 MB account or you want write parallelism today.
  3. Your reads are hot enough that off-chain Merkle proofs would add unacceptable latency.

If two of three apply, Torna is still likely the right choice. If only one applies, you might be better served by the targeted alternative.

The bigger picture

The Solana primitive layer is unevenly developed. Token-2022, account compression, address lookup tables — these are mature primitives that programs reuse without rethinking. Sorted storage has been a hole. Every team filled it themselves, which means the ecosystem has a half-dozen incompatible slab allocators and no shared substrate.

A shared substrate is what Torna is positioned to become. The market opportunity isn't just one DEX — it's every protocol that needs sorted storage discovering that the primitive already exists and CPI-ing into it.
